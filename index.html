  • Uproot (2017)

    Product Design

    Uproot is an agriculture startup aimed at growing spinach in a fully automated vertical aeroponic garden. Our group wanted to show that it is possible to have a steady supply of fresh produce all year round by using modern agricultural technology.

    The idea behind Uproot is addressing the value of animals in a contemporary society. The project is tackling how humans view animals as property instead of equals, how our society places infinite value on a human life yet disregard the same intrinsic right for animals and instead devalue it by focusing on their commercial and personal value. Philosophy of veganism and exploration of speciesism, prejudice in favour of the interests of members of one's own species, and anthropocentrism, regarding humankind as the central element of existence, have always been the central focus of this project.

    An Arduino web server was used to host the dashboard interface. The project was designed to be shown in a public setting so this was the best option, being relatively quick to setup and allowing multiple users to view the local server from their own devices, creating a more intimate experience.

  • SOMA (2017)

    DevArt

    SOMA is an interactive video installation that lets audience participants to scrub through projected video footage using a large lever. The installation focuses on themes of control and balance, and is inspired by the experiences of the characters John and Lenina in Aldous Huxley’s dystopian science fiction novel, Brave New World (1932).

    SOMA is the product of an iterative, team process, informally structured around cycles of group discussion, prototype development and critique. Parallel to this process, research was conducted to provide context for both the artistic concept and the technology approach.

    The video content of two children - brother and sister Luca (9) and Elisa (11) Joint-Rotondo - shows the pair being brought in and out of a large, cold pool of water. The audience is presented with a choice as to which child they put above water and which they sink below. A balance can be achieved with both children half submerged, but only at the threshold between states, leaving the audience to choose if they will set the children into cycles of transient relief, or leave them in a state of compromise.

  • Flattie (2016)

    UX/UIDev

    Flattie is a data visualisation web app aimed at assisting students in auckland with the challenges in finding a place to live.

    Many government data sources are openly available, however they can be overwhelming and tricky to comprehend. flattie helps to bring this data in, and display it in the simplest way possible without compromising consumer usability. our rental data was supplied by the ministry of business, innovation and employment, who show the change of rental prices over time in different areas in new zealand in a csv file.

    Flattie helps minimise the strain in the first stage of finding a place. after navigating to www.flattie.space, the user is presented with a map of auckland, and some brief information to start using the web app. hovering over a specific auckland suburb brings up a info bubble. clicking on it shows more information about the suburb, including quick links to trade me for that given suburb.

  • SpotHype (2016)

    UX/UI

    Winner of the "Best project" award at an Auckland Summer of Tech Hackfest 2016. Designed and built in 48 hours.

    “SpotHype” is a web application for businesses interested in better understanding customer sentiment towards their products and services based on location. It enables businesses to more accurately target their marketing based on location and user sentiment, enabling them to produce greater ROI from their limited marketing budget. For example, businesses wanting to understand customer reactions to new product launches, marketing campaigns, and events.

    This app is a map of customer reactions through twitter and other social media. Using a colour-coded heat-map to represent sentiment (red being negative, green positive, and yellow neutral).

  • Podlink (2016)

    Product DesignDev

    PodLink started as a touch screen kiosk redesign but went through multiple iterations until it was decided to focus on touch screen interfaces for use in transportation. To create a transparent and interactive display several technologies were investigated. Unfortunately the most relevant technologies, such as capacitive OLED displays are far out of reach in terms of its current technological state and price. A compromise, is to simulate this new technology using affordable and available resources. We decided upon an optical based touch screen, which is a similar technology to that which powers Microsoft’s Surface tabletop multitouch display using an array of 40 Infrared LEDs and an infrared camera, a users finger is tracked as it comes in contact with with a sheet of acrylic, creating an interactive multitouch surface. Behind the arylic is a transparent rear projection film which allows the display to remain transparent while being projected upon. We see this type of technology being implemented in bus stops windows, for displaying up to date bus routes and other relevant info.

    The display uses 40 infrared LEDs, an infrared webcam, acrylic and image and finger blob processing software. Placed around 3 sides of the 10mm acrylic are the LEDs that face directly inwards. Frustrated total internal reflection (FTIR) is the phenomenon in which light inside of media remains reflecting upon the media until the index of refraction of another object in contact, in this case a finger, is presented , allowing the light to reflect off of the finger which creates a blob of light under it. To accomplish this effect a relatively thick acrylic sheet was required, and LEDs were faced inwards and pressed against a finely polished acrylic edge to allow for maximum light penetration.

  • Telephony (2015)

    Product DesignDev

    In The Design of Everyday Things (1998), Don Norman established the principle that good human-centric designs are intuitive. That is, the way an object is designed should tell you clearly what you are able to do with it.

    The TELEPHONY is a project which seeks to take this fundamental intuitiveness and misdirect it. It examines the universally familiar design of the telephone and subverts its functionality, thus creating a disconnect between the user's expectations and the object's behaviour. Various responses to this are revealed, ranging from frustration to fascination, confusion to embarrassment. Ultimately we are encouraged to question the way we have come to understand everyday objects, and the inherently manipulative role of the designer.

    The TELEPHONY is programmed with Arduino and the repurposed internal electronic components of a 1986 Telecom phone model.
